📊 How Merge Can Mislead

Real Lessons from a Sales Data Project with Pandas

Introduction

Merging datasets is one of the most common tasks in data science. But there’s a hidden trap when you join summary (aggregated) data into detailed records.

This blog shows a real mistake I made and the surprising lesson I learned using pandas.merge().

🔧 Project Setup

  • sales.csv – Row-level sales data from customers
  • products.csv – Product details + total quantity sold (pre-aggregated)
Goal: Calculate Expected Revenue by customer, product, and month.

🚨 The Mistake

I merged products into sales and calculated revenue like this:

merged_df = pd.merge(sales_df, products_df, on="Product", how="left")
merged_df["Expected_Revenue"] = merged_df["Price"] * merged_df["Quantity"]
❗ Problem: The Quantity column from products.csv was pre-aggregated.
Every sales row repeated the total quantity, inflating revenue.
"When you merge a table containing totals into detailed data, pandas repeats them — causing double-counting."

✅ The Fix

  • Don’t merge in totals — only use per-product info like Price
  • Do revenue calculation in sales.csv where quantity is actual per order
# Correct way (if Quantity is per order):
merged_df['Expected_Revenue'] = merged_df['Price'] * merged_df['Quantity']

🧠 What I Learned

❌ Don’t mix grouped data with raw data unless you’re sure.
✅ Always check row counts before and after a merge.
🧠 Pandas is powerful — but silent. It won’t warn you!
